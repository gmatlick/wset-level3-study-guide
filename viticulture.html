Viticulture & Winemaking

Learning Outcome 1: Natural and Human Factors

The Vine

Species & Varieties

  • Vitis vinifera: European wine species
  • Varieties: Cabernet Sauvignon, Chardonnay, etc.
  • Clones: Variants within a variety
  • Crossings: Two V. vinifera parents
  • Hybrids: V. vinifera crossed with other species

Vine Structure

  • Green parts: Shoots, leaves, tendrils
  • One-year-old wood: Canes from last season
  • Permanent wood: Trunk and arms
  • Rootstock: Root system (often non-vinifera)

Vine Needs

  • Warmth: For photosynthesis and ripening
  • Sunlight: For photosynthesis
  • Water: For growth and sugar accumulation
  • Nutrients: Nitrogen, potassium, phosphorus
  • Carbon dioxide: For photosynthesis

Vine Growth Cycle

  • Dormancy: Winter rest period
  • Budburst: Spring awakening
  • Flowering: Fruit set begins
  • Fruit set: Berries form
  • Véraison: Grapes change color, begin ripening
  • Ripening: Sugar accumulation, acid decrease
Exam Tip: Understand how disruptions at each growth stage affect yield and quality. For example, poor fruit set leads to fewer berries but potentially higher quality.

Climate

Climate Type Temperature Range Characteristics Example Regions
Cool 16.5°C or below (mean growing season) Light body, high acidity, lower alcohol, fresh fruit Chablis, Mosel, Marlborough
Moderate 16.5-18.5°C Medium body, balanced, fresh to cooked fruit Bordeaux, Burgundy, Northern Rhône
Warm 18.5-21°C Full body, lower acidity, higher alcohol, ripe fruit Southern Rhône, Rioja, Barossa
Hot Above 21°C Very full, low acidity, high alcohol, dried fruit Jerez, Central Valley (Chile), parts of Australia

Climate Classifications

  • Mediterranean: Hot, dry summers; mild, wet winters
  • Maritime: Moderate temp, high rainfall, low diurnal range
  • Continental: Hot summers, cold winters, high diurnal range

Factors Influencing Regional Climate

  • Latitude: 30-50° N/S ideal
  • Altitude: Higher = cooler (6°C per 1000m)
  • Oceans: Moderate temperatures
  • Large lakes: Moderate local climate
  • Continentality: Distance from moderating water
  • Diurnal range: Day-night temperature difference

Site Climate (Mesoclimate)

  • Aspect: Slope direction affects sun exposure
  • Soil: Dark soils absorb heat, light soils reflect
  • Drainage: Well-drained soils warm faster
  • Fog: Reduces temperature, affects botrytis
  • Water bodies: Reflect sunlight, moderate temp

Weather vs. Vintage Variation

  • Short-term atmospheric conditions
  • Affects each vintage differently
  • Frost, hail, rain during harvest
  • Exceptional heat or cold spells
  • Drought or excessive rainfall
Exam Tip: Always connect climate to style, quality, and price. Cool climates = higher acidity = better for sparkling. Warm climates = riper fruit = fuller body wines.

Soil

Key Functions of Soil

  • Water supply: Drainage is crucial - waterlogged roots die
  • Nutrients: Nitrogen (growth), potassium (fruit), phosphorus (ripening)
  • Heat retention: Stony soils absorb heat during day, release at night
Important: Soil TYPE is less important than soil PROPERTIES. Focus on drainage, nutrients, and heat retention rather than memorizing specific soil names (unless linked to specific appellations like Chablis Kimmeridgian limestone).

Vineyard Management

Training System Description Advantages Examples
Head-trained Little permanent wood, spur-pruned Low cost, suits poor soils, traditional Gobelet, bush vines
Cordon-trained One or more horizontal arms Suits mechanization, consistent Guyot, VSP (Vertical Shoot Positioning)

Canopy Management

  • Goals: Optimize light, air, temperature
  • Techniques: Leaf removal, shoot positioning, topping
  • Benefits: Reduces disease, improves ripening, controls vigor

Planting Density

  • High density (>5000/ha): Competition, lower yields, higher quality
  • Low density (<2000/ha): Suitable for mechanization, fertile soils
  • Burgundy: 10,000 vines/ha typical
  • New World: 2,000-3,000 vines/ha typical

Harvest

  • Timing: Balance sugar, acidity, phenolic ripeness
  • Manual: Selective, gentler, slopes, expensive
  • Machine: Fast, cheap, flat land, less selective

Hazards & Management

Frost

Problem: Kills young shoots in spring

Solutions:

  • Site selection (hillsides, not valleys)
  • Heaters (oil/gas burners)
  • Wind machines (mix air layers)
  • Sprinklers (ice protects bud)

Hail

Problem: Damages fruit and leaves

Solutions:

  • Netting (expensive)
  • Multiple vineyard sites (diversification)
  • Hail rockets (controversial effectiveness)

Water Stress

Problem: Drought stops growth/ripening

Solutions:

  • Irrigation: drip (efficient, expensive)
  • Sprinkler (cheaper, less efficient)
  • Flood (traditional, wasteful)

Sunburn

Problem: Grapes damaged by excessive sun

Solutions:

  • Canopy management (partial shade)
  • Leaf retention on sunny side
  • Row orientation (N-S vs E-W)

Pests

Phylloxera: Louse attacks roots

  • Solution: Graft vinifera onto resistant American rootstocks

Other: Birds, deer, wild boar, insects

  • Netting, fencing, insecticides

Diseases

Fungal: Powdery mildew, downy mildew, botrytis

  • Canopy management (air circulation)
  • Fungicides (preventative spraying)

Viral/Bacterial: Leafroll, fanleaf

  • No cure - replant vineyard
  • Sanitize equipment

Vineyard Management Approaches

Approach Principles Advantages Challenges
Conventional Use synthetic chemicals, focused on productivity Reliable, lower labor, cost-effective Environmental impact, chemical residues
Sustainable Minimize intervention, integrated pest management Balance productivity and environment Requires expertise, monitoring
Organic No synthetic chemicals, natural alternatives only Better for environment and health More labor, risk in wet climates, expensive
Biodynamic Holistic, cosmic calendar, preparations Vineyard as self-sustaining ecosystem Highly labor-intensive, costly, requires belief

Concentrating Grape Sugars

Noble Rot (Botrytis cinerea)

Fungus punctures grape skin, water evaporates, concentrates sugars

  • Requires: Misty mornings, sunny afternoons
  • Examples: Sauternes, Tokaji Aszú, German TBA/BA
  • Impact: Honey, dried fruit flavors; high cost

Drying on the Vine

Leave grapes to raisinate on vine

  • Examples: Amarone, some Jurançon
  • Impact: Dried fruit, higher alcohol, full body

Drying After Picking

Dry harvested grapes on mats or racks

  • Examples: Passito wines, Recioto, some Vin Santo
  • Duration: Weeks to months
  • Impact: Concentrated, dried fruit character

Freezing on the Vine

Grapes frozen naturally, harvested at -8°C or below

  • Examples: Icewine (Canada), Eiswein (Germany/Austria)
  • Impact: Intense sweetness, high acidity, expensive
  • Risk: Weather dependent, birds, rot
