THE ANNUAL VINE CYCLE

1Ô∏è‚É£ Winter Dormancy

Timing: December-March (Northern Hemisphere) / June-September (Southern Hemisphere)

What happens: The vine becomes dormant as temperatures drop. Shoots become woody (canes). Leaves fall and vine stores carbohydrates in roots.

Vineyard tasks: Winter pruning takes place. Prune back previous year's growth to prepare for next season.

Critical factor: Vines can withstand cold temperatures (down to -15¬∞C to -20¬∞C), but extreme cold can kill the vine. In continental climates, earth may be piled around base for protection.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Budburst

Timing: March-April (NH) / September-October (SH)

Temperature trigger: Mean daily temperature reaches 10¬∞C (50¬∞F)

What happens: Buds swell and burst, growing into new shoots. This is the start of the growing season and annually begins when vine sap starts to rise.

Grape variety factor: Some varieties bud early (Chardonnay, Pinot Noir) while others bud late (Cabernet Sauvignon). Early-budding varieties risk spring frost damage.

Critical risks: Spring frost can kill new shoots. Frost protection methods include heaters, wind machines, sprinklers ("aspersion").

3Ô∏è‚É£ Early Shoot and Leaf Growth

Timing: March-May (NH) / September-November (SH)

What happens: Shoots grow rapidly, vine flowers are not yet apparent. Leaves emerge and begin photosynthesis. The vine needs a good supply of water and sufficient warmth early in season. Carbohydrate stores provide initial energy. Frost risk remains.

Vineyard tasks: Spraying continues as necessary for pest/disease control.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Flowering and Fruit Set

Flowering timing: May-June (NH) / November-December (SH)

Temperature requirement: Vines start flowering when inflorescences (bunches of flowers) become warm enough (typically above 17¬∞C). Warm temperatures, plenty of sunshine, and little to no rain are ideal during flowering.

Pollination: Grapevines are self-pollinating. Wind carries pollen within the vine.

Fruit set: Occurs when a flower develops into a grape. Not many flowers than normal fail to fertilize this condition is called coulure (poor fruit set). If many flowers drop off if there are too many flowers, reducing fruit set. Both of these conditions reduce yields and usually stem from cold, cloudy or rainy weather during flowering.

Critical factor: Weather during 2-week flowering period determines potential crop size.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Grape Development - Early Stages

Timing: June-July (NH) / December-January (SH)

What happens: Grapes form and grow. Grapes are hard, green, and high in acid. Photosynthesis continues, sugar levels slowly increase.

Vineyard management: Green harvest (crop thinning) may take place to remove excess bunches and improve ripening of remaining fruit. Summer pruning takes place to remove excess foliage and improve air circulation.

6Ô∏è‚É£ V√©raison and Berry Ripening

V√©raison timing: July-September (NH) / January-March (SH)

Definition: V√©raison marks the onset of ripening at which time the grapes begin to ripen. Black varieties change color (from green to black/blue/purple), white grapes become translucent and golden.

Chemical changes during ripening:

  • Sugar levels rise dramatically
  • Acidity drops (especially malic acid)
  • Tannins ripen (become less bitter)
  • Color compounds develop in skins (anthocyanins for red/black grapes)
  • Aroma/flavor compounds develop

Warm temperatures ideal: Mild water stress aids ripening. If watered, summer pruning takes place to remove excess foliage (ensuring the removal of a proportion of grape bunches) may take place shortly after v√©raison to control yield and improve fruit quality. Not all grape growers use this technique.

Spraying continues as necessary

7Ô∏è‚É£ Harvest

Timing: September-October (NH) / March-April (SH)

Decision factors: Grower must decide ideal harvest time by monitoring:

  • Sugar levels (measured in ¬∞Brix, ¬∞Oechsle, or ¬∞Baum√©)
  • Acidity levels
  • Tannin ripeness (for reds)
  • Flavor development
  • Weather forecast (rain before harvest can dilute juice and cause rot)

Harvest methods:

Machine harvesting: Fast, cheap, works 24 hours. Can be used for grapes that will be sorted. Not suitable for whole-bunch pressing, steep slopes, or premium wines requiring selection.

Hand harvesting: Essential for whole bunches, steep slopes, premium quality, selective picking (noble rot, ice wine). Slow and expensive but provides selection in vineyard.

Ideal conditions: No rainfall before harvest. Dry conditions limit rot risk. For sweet wines (botrytis), multiple selective pickings ("tries") necessary.

WINEMAKING PROCESSES

WHITE WINE PRODUCTION

1. Crushing & Pressing

Crushing: Grapes crushed to break skins and release juice. Not always done.

Pressing: Juice separated from skins immediately (for white wines). Gentle pressing important for quality - avoid extracting bitter compounds from skins/seeds.

Press types: Pneumatic press (air bag), basket press, continuous screw press (lower quality)

2. Clarification (Settling)

Juice settles in tank, solids (lees) drop to bottom. Clean juice racked off. Some producers ferment "on solids" for texture/complexity.

3. Alcoholic Fermentation

Temperature: 12-22¬∞C (54-72¬∞F) - cool temperatures preserve primary fruit aromas

Vessel: Stainless steel (neutral, temperature control) or oak (adds flavor, allows micro-oxygenation)

Yeast: Cultured yeast (reliable, selected flavors) or ambient yeast (terroir expression, risk)

Duration: Typically 2-4 weeks depending on temperature and yeast

4. Post-Fermentation Choices

Malolactic conversion (optional): Convert sharp malic acid ‚Üí softer lactic acid. Creates buttery, creamy texture. Common in Chardonnay, not used for crisp styles (Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc).

Lees contact: Wine left on lees (dead yeast cells). Adds texture, complexity, breadth. Lees stirring ("b√¢tonnage") increases effect.

Sur lie aging: Extended lees contact (Muscadet, premium Champagne)

5. Maturation

Stainless steel: Preserves fruit, no oak influence, fresh style

Oak barrels: Adds vanilla, toast, spice flavors. Allows micro-oxygenation (softens wine). 225L barrique standard. New oak = more flavor. Old oak = neutral.

Oak alternatives: Oak chips, staves (cheaper than barrels)

6. Clarification & Stabilization

Fining: Protein added (egg white, bentonite) binds to particles, removed

Filtering: Wine passed through filter to remove particles

Cold stabilization: Chill wine to precipitate tartrate crystals (harmless but look like glass)

7. Bottling

Sulfur dioxide (SO‚ÇÇ) added to prevent oxidation and microbial spoilage. Wine bottled under sterile conditions.

RED WINE PRODUCTION

1. Crushing & Destemming

Destemming: Remove stems (contain bitter tannins). Some producers use whole-bunch fermentation for premium wines (adds complexity, silky tannins, herbaceous notes).

Crushing: Break skins to release juice and allow fermentation to begin.

2. Pre-Fermentation Maceration (Optional)

Cold maceration: Crushed grapes held at cold temperature (4-10¬∞C) before fermentation. Extracts color and flavor without tannin. Common in Pinot Noir.

3. Alcoholic Fermentation

Key difference from white: Fermentation occurs WITH skins present (for color, tannin, flavor extraction)

Temperature: 20-32¬∞C (68-90¬∞F) - warmer than whites

Duration: 5-14 days typically (longer = more extraction)

Cap management: Skins float to top forming "cap". Must be pushed down or pumped over to extract color/tannin:

  • Punching down (pigeage): Manual or mechanical plunging of cap
  • Pumping over (remontage): Pump juice from bottom over cap
  • Rack and return: Drain juice, return over cap (aggressive)
  • Rotary fermenters: Rotating tank keeps skins in contact with juice

4. Pressing

After fermentation, free-run wine is drained off. Skins pressed to extract press wine (more tannic, colored). Press wine may be blended back or aged separately.

5. Malolactic Conversion

ALWAYS used in red wines (unlike whites where it's optional). Softens acidity, adds complexity. Occurs at 18-22¬∞C.

6. Maturation

Oak aging: Most reds aged in oak. Softens tannins, adds complexity. Duration varies: 6 months to 3+ years depending on wine style.

Micro-oxygenation: Controlled oxygen exposure softens tannins without barrel aging (used for mid-priced wines).

7. Clarification & Bottling

Fining and filtering (optional - some premium wines unfiltered). Bottle aging often required before release.

SWEET WINE PRODUCTION

Fundamental principle: Sweet wines have residual sugar (sugar not converted to alcohol during fermentation)

Method 1: Concentrating Grape Sugars

A) Noble Rot (Botrytis cinerea)

Conditions: Humid misty mornings (fungus grows) + dry sunny afternoons (fungus stops, grapes shrivel). Example: Sauternes (Ciron river mist).

Effect: Botrytis punctures skin, water evaporates, concentrates sugar/acid/flavor. Creates unique honey, dried apricot, marmalade flavors.

Harvest: Multiple selective pickings ("tries") of only botrytized grapes. Very low yields. Expensive.

Examples: Sauternes, Tokaji, Beerenauslese/Trockenbeerenauslese

B) Drying Grapes on the Vine (Passerillage)

Process: Leave grapes on vine past normal harvest. Late harvest concentrates sugars.

Examples: Vendange Tardive (Alsace), Sp√§tlese/Auslese (Germany)

C) Drying Grapes After Picking

Process: Grapes dried on mats or hung in well-ventilated rooms for weeks/months. Water evaporates, sugar concentrates.

Flavors: Raisined fruit, fig, dried apricot

Examples: Recioto della Valpolicella, Vin Santo, Pedro Xim√©nez Sherry

D) Freezing Grapes (Ice Wine / Eiswein)

Process: Grapes left on vine into winter, picked and pressed while frozen (typically -8¬∞C or below). Water remains frozen, concentrated juice extracted.

Result: Intense sweetness, high acidity (perfect balance), fresh fruit flavors

Examples: German/Austrian Eiswein, Canadian Icewine, Finger Lakes
