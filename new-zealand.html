Benchmark Sauvignon Blanc and elegant Pinot Noir

New Zealand is best known for fresh, vibrant Sauvignon Blanc that has set the global benchmark. However, the country also produces premium Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Syrah, and Bordeaux-style blends, showing there is far more to NZ than one grape variety.

New Zealand GI System

Recently established GI scheme, first GIs registered in 2017. Minimum 85% of grapes must come from the stated region. New Zealand, North Island, and South Island are registered GIs, along with all major regions detailed below.

Climate & Grape Growing

üå§Ô∏è Climate

Maritime climate overall (1900km from Australia). South Island: Cool. North Island: Warmer. Long sunshine hours, nights cooled by sea breezes, long ripening period allows high sugar/flavor ripeness while retaining acidity. Vineyards on South Island's east side, protected by central mountains from westerly rain. Plentiful rainfall can be problematic, but free-draining soils prevent waterlogging.

üå± Viticultural Challenges

Flat land can be overly fertile, leading to excessive shoot/leaf growth. NZ viticulturists have become experts in trellising and canopy management. Sustainable Winegrowing New Zealand initiative sets standards to reduce environmental impact.

Grape Varieties & Winemaking

üçá White Varieties

Sauvignon Blanc (flagship grape, majority of production): Pungent aromas, intense elderflower/passionfruit flavors, high acidity. Set global benchmark. North Island: tropical flavors. South Island: higher acidity, green bell pepper, gooseberry. Usually fermented in inert vessels at cool temps. Some oak-matured styles.

Chardonnay: Widely planted. Diverse styles. Concentrated citrus/tropical fruits with subtle toast from new French oak.

Riesling, Gew√ºrztraminer, Pinot Gris: Enormous potential. Dry autumns, cool nights develop aromatic compounds. Dry to sweet, off-dry most prevalent.

üçá Black Varieties

Pinot Noir (2nd most planted after Sauvignon Blanc): Widely grown throughout South Island and select North Island sites. Vibrant fruit, fine ripe tannins, often high alcohol. Distinct regional styles emerging.

Merlot: Red variety with most plantings and volume (after Pinot). Often blended with Cabernet Sauvignon for tannin/cassis (Bordeaux-style). Purity of fruit. Concentrated in North Island where warmer temps help ripen blacks.

Syrah: Performs best in warmer sites. Elegant wines closer to Rh√¥ne style than hot-climate Australia. Small but increasing plantings.

North Island Regions

Auckland

Traditional heart of NZ wine industry. Many large companies retain headquarters here but production often elsewhere. Warm and wettest part of NZ - fungal diseases are an issue. Chardonnay, Merlot, Syrah most planted. Waiheke Island (east) has growing reputation for top-quality wines, particularly reds.

Gisborne

East coast, broad estuary plain. High rainfall but also high temps and sunshine hours in growing season. Chardonnay by far most planted: Distinct ripe tropical fruit flavors. Small amount of high-quality Gew√ºrztraminer and full-bodied Pinot Gris.

Hawke's Bay

Near Hastings and Napier, east coast estuary plain. Warmest main grape-growing area, longest sunshine hours. Second only to Marlborough in plantings. Diverse soils, aspects, altitudes create varied styles. Gimblett Gravels: Well-drained heat-absorbing gravel soil, particularly high reputation for black varieties. Famous for Merlot and Cabernet Sauvignon (Bordeaux-style blends). Premium Syrah also produced.

Wairarapa (Martinborough)

Southern tip of North Island. Small vineyard areas spread over wide area. Martinborough most important, registered GI, global reputation for Pinot Noir. High summer temps but wide diurnal range. Pinot Noir: Medium-to-full body, ripe, dark plum, spice.

South Island Regions

Marlborough (Major Centre)

Major center for grape growing in NZ. Sauvignon Blanc makes up majority of plantings. Most vineyards in two adjacent estuary valleys:

Wairau Valley (by far largest): Long sunny days. Large valley, not uniform. Side valleys on southern side give range of aspects/altitudes.
Awatere Valley: Drier, cooler, windier. Sauvignon Blanc has higher acidity, pronounced herbaceous character, lacks tropical fruit of Wairau.

Chardonnay and Pinot Noir also widely planted: Used for still and sparkling wine. Pinot Noir: medium body, fine tannins, cherry/cranberry. Increasingly impressive as producers focus on variety. Riesling and Pinot Gris perform well.

Nelson

NW of Wairau Valley, north coast of South Island. Cooler and wetter than Marlborough. Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Noir, Pinot Gris dominate plantings.

Canterbury (includes Waipara Valley)

Two main centers: Plains west of Christchurch and larger Waipara Valley to north. Plains exposed to cooling Pacific but warming from NW winds. Waipara: More warming, less ocean exposure (hills). Sauvignon Blanc and Pinot Noir most planted. Waipara noted for high-quality Riesling.

Central Otago

Inland in foothills of Southern Alps. Spread over wide area with different valleys, aspects, soils, altitudes. Continental climate (contrast to rest of NZ). Frost risk spring and autumn. Summers warm but large diurnal range. Very high sunlight intensity = high alcohol. Pinot Noir dominates (vast majority of plantings): Full-bodied, juicy, vibrant, concentrated ripe red fruit. Also Pinot Gris, Riesling, Chardonnay.
