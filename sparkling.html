The other important varieties are Pinot Gris, Riesling and Chardonnay.

The Vineyard

The requirements for growing grapes for sparkling wine are quite different from those needed to produce grapes for still wine. First, the grapes should ideally be relatively low in sugar compared with those used for still wines. The second fermentation that gives most sparkling wines their bubbles also increases the alcohol levels of the wines by a further 1.2-1.3% abv. Therefore winemakers will generally aim to produce a still dry base wine with 10-11% abv. Second, high acid levels are desired for many sparkling wines, which are made in a refreshing style. Despite the relatively low sugar concentration and high acid levels, the flavours of the grapes must still be sufficiently ripe so that they no longer have any green herbaceous flavour characteristics.

Meeting these requirements can be difficult in all but the coolest regions. In cool regions the changes to the sugar and acid levels happen slowly and at the same time as the changes to the grapes' flavours. However, in warm regions sugar levels can rise and acid levels fall very quickly. Picking early to get the correct levels of sugar and acid is the only option. However, this often means picking grapes that are still overly green and herbaceous. Ultimately, finding an area with a sufficiently cool climate is fundamental to growing grapes for premium sparkling wine production.

Handling The Grapes And Juice

To produce high-quality sparkling wines it is vital that the grapes and their juice are treated with care. Harvest The grapes for the best sparkling wines are typically hand-picked to retain whole bunches of unsplit grapes. However, in warm regions where grapes are being grown for the production of inexpensive sparkling wines, machine harvesting may be the most efficient way of picking all the grapes before they accumulate too much sugar and lose their acidity. Pressing avoiding crushing, contact between the skins and juice is minimised. In most EU regions legislation determines the maximum pressure that can be used during pressing and the amount of juice that can be extracted from the grapes.

On arrival at the winery the grapes should be pressed as soon as possible. Pressing will be gentle, to minimise the extraction of any tannins and colour. This is especially important when using black grapes for white sparkling wines. The use of whole bunches helps with this. Blending - The blending process is very important in the making of sparkling wines and serves a number of functions. First, many producers aim to make their sparkling wines in a particular house style, particularly for non-vintage products. Blending wines from different vineyards, grape varieties and/or vintages can help to achieve this house style year-on-year despite vintage variation

Second, blending can be used to improve the balance of the wine. For example, Chardonnay is widely considered to bring citrus fruit, finesse and longevity to a blend, whereas Pinot Noir brings more body. Third, blending can enhance the complexity of the wine. Old reserve wines can bring flavours of dried fruit to the fresh flavours of the young wine. It is easier to achieve these aims if blenders have as many wines as possible at their disposal. For this reason grapes are often fermented in many different small parcels, giving multiple blending options.

Second alcoholic fermentation - Once the blend is made up, a small proportion of liqueur de tirage is added. This is a mixture of wine, sugar, yeast, yeast nutrients and a clarifying agent. The bottle is closed with a crown cap that includes a plastic cup insert. The bottles are then stacked horizontally in the producer's cellars at a cool, constant temperature. A slow fermentation takes place. The alcohol is raised by around 1.2-1.3% abv and the CO, generated by the yeast dissolves into the wine creating the sparkle. This creates pressure in the bottle equivalent to five to six atmospheres. Yeast autolysis - Once this second alcoholic fermentation is complete the yeast die and form a sediment of lees in the bottle. Over a period of months these dead yeast cells start to break down releasing chemical compounds into the wine, a process known as yeast autolysis. These compounds contribute to the flavour of the wine, typically giving bread, biscuit and toasty notes. Autolysis is usually most active over the first four to five years, but has been known to continue for several more years. Wines that spend longer on their lees normally develop a more pronounced yeast character. Once autolysis is complete, keeping the lees in contact with the wine can maintain the wine's freshness for years, however, in most cases, maturation

will not last that long, with the yeast removed before autolysis is complete. Riddling - After the period of maturation, the lees sediment is removed using the two processes of riddling and disgorgement. Riddling involves moving the bottle very slowly from a horizontal to an inverted vertical position. The yeast sediment is dislodged and gradually slides down the side of the bottle collecting in the plastic cup insert in the crown cap. Traditionally, this was done by hand. The bottles were placed horizontally in the holes of an A-frame rack called a pupitre and then each day they were given a gentle shake and a twist and raised slightly closer to vertical. This was a very labour intensive process and took up to eight weeks to complete.

Nowadays riddling is usually mechanised so that the work can be carried out faster and in bulk. The standard machine in use today is a gyropalette, a cage holding 500 bottles on a hydraulic arm, which is regularly rotated and inclined. This simulates hand riddling but completes the job in a matter of days. Disgorgement and corking - Once riddling is complete the neck of the upturned bottle is submerged in a very cold solution, which freezes the wine in the neck. The bottles are then inverted into a normal upright position keeping the wine clear. The crown cap seal is removed and the frozen wine holds the sediment in place, and the pressure created by the dissolved CO, ejects the frozen wine taking the sediment and plastic insert with it. The wine is topped up with liqueur d'expédition and sealed with a cork that is held secure by a wire cage. This whole process is now fully mechanised and the time taken from opening to resealing the bottle is a matter of seconds. This avoids a loss of pressure and reduces the risk of oxidation,

Liqueur d'expédition is a mixture of wine and sugar. The amount of sugar used will determine the final level of sweetness in the wine and is often known as the dosage. It is used to balance the acidity and it helps with flavour development. There are some wines that are made without the addition of sugar at this stage. These wines are typically labelled Brut nature or Zéro dosage depending on the producer and wine region (for additional terms see Styles of Sparkling Wine later in this chapter). A small proportion of wine will still be added to top up the sparkling wine to the right level in the bottle.

The characteristics of the wine within the liqueur d'expédition are very important. For example, the wine may be young and fresh, toasty from maturation in oak, or display dried fruit flavours from years in bottle. In effect this is a second blending operation used to create the house style. The vast majority of sparkling wines (made by any method) are sealed with a cork. Even with this degree of compression, a wire cage is added for complete security.

Styles Of Sparkling Wine

Sparkling wines are made in a range of sweetness levels. Those made within the EU or made for sale within the EU must use certain labelling terms to denote the level of sweetness (see table EU Labelling Terms for Sweetness in Sparkling Wines). It is important to note that there is a range of sweetness levels allowed for each description. Some producers will use the lower end of the range and some the higher end. This contributes to different producers' house styles. Many of these sweetness labelling terms are widely copied around the globe but only need to comply with these regulations if the wine is being sold in the EU,

The following terms are used to describe styles of sparkling wines: Non-vintage-This is not a labelling term, but it is used to describe wines made from grapes harvested in more than one vintage. These wines tend to represent the standard offering from the producer and display the house style. Vintage-In Champagne, this term means that the wine must come from a single year. However, some appellations In other regions may allow a small percentage of grapes from other years to be included in the blend. In regions that experience considerable vintage variation, such as Champagne, vintage wines will only be made in the very

best years, and thus command a price premium. In other regions, vintage wines may be made more frequently, and may not be held in quite the same esteem. Rosé - Rosé sparkling wines can either be made by blending red and white base wines or by a short maceration. Colour can also be adjusted with the liqueur d'expédition. There are some sparkling wine appellations that only permit their rosé wines to be made by maceration. Blanc de Blancs - A white sparkling wine made only from white grape varieties. Blanc de Noirs - A white sparkling wine made only from black grape varieties. Prestige Cuvée - This is also not a labelling term but generally describes the best wine(s) in a producer's range. Although small in number, these wines are an important part of the sparkling wine market. This is particularly true of Champagne, where reputations have been built based on an association with luxury as well as celebration.

Champagne

Champagne consists of a single appellation. Certain villages within the region have a cru status; 44 are rated premier cru and 17 grand cru, but these are not separate appellations. Unlike in Burgundy or Alsace, it is the whole village that is rated and not specific vineyard sites, and the growers in these villages are not required to meet stricter production requirements such as lower yields Inevitably, there will be some outstanding sites within villages that have lower ratings. Equally, not every plot within a village rated grand cru will have the same potential. Nevertheless, this classification persists and these terms are permitted on Champagne labels.

Within the appellation boundaries there are five sub-regions where there are major concentrations of vineyards. These are spread over a considerable area. The three most famous sub-regions, which are close to the major production centres of Reims and Épernay, are Montagne de Reims, Vallée de la Marne and the Côte des Blancs. The Côte de Sézanne lies approximately 30 kilometres south-west of Épernay and the Côte des Bar is over 100 kilometres to the south-east. Climate and Grape Growing Champagne has a cool continental climate. Viticulture is challenging and growers regularly have to contend with winter freeze, spring frosts and rainy, cloudy weather. However, even in the warmest years grape sugar levels remain low and acid levels high, which makes this region ideal for sparkling wine production.

Frost is one of the biggest challenges in the vineyard. In order to minimise this threat most vineyards are planted on slopes. The soils in Champagne are dominated by chalk, which provides good drainage after rain storms but retains a sufficient quantity of water in dry periods. Champagne's producers are now fully committed to sustainable agriculture and have ambitious targets to reduce their use of man-made fertilisers and pesticides.

New Zealand

New Zealand has an established reputation for high- quality traditional method sparkling wines made from the classic Champagne grape varieties. Its relatively southerly position in the Southern Hemisphere and maritime climate with cool sea breezes means that it can ripen Chardonnay and Pinot Noir slowly enough to produce elegant sparkling wines. A range of styles is made including non-vintage, vintage, rosé, Blanc de Blancs and Blanc de Noirs. Grapes for sparkling wine production are grown in all the wine regions, however, Marlborough produces the highest volumes. The warmer regions in North Island produce a richer style compared with the wines from the South Island and producers often take advantage of this fact making cross regional blends.

Sparkling Sauvignon Blanc is also produced, usually by carbonation or the tank method to retain the intense aromas and flavours of this grape variety.
