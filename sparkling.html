Traditional Method & Global Styles

Growing Grapes for Sparkling Wine

Key Requirements: Low sugar (base wine 10-11% ABV before 2nd fermentation adds 1.2-1.3%), high acidity, ripe flavors without herbaceous character. Cool climates ideal - sugar/acid changes happen slowly alongside flavor development. Warm regions: pick early but risk green/herbaceous flavors.

üçá Harvest

Premium: Hand-picked (whole bunches, unsplit grapes). Inexpensive/warm regions: Machine harvesting (pick before too much sugar/acid loss).

üçæ Pressing

Gentle pressing ASAP. Minimize tannin/color extraction (important for white sparkling from black grapes). Whole bunches help. EU regions limit pressure and juice extraction.

Traditional Method (M√©thode Traditionnelle)

Second fermentation in bottle where wine is sold. Most premium sparkling wines use this method. Costly and time-consuming but produces highest prices.

Traditional Method Steps

1. Base Wine

First alcoholic fermentation in stainless steel (or oak for some). Completely dry, neutral flavors, high acidity. May/may not undergo MLF or oak maturation. Most base wines used year after harvest. Some stored as reserve wines for blending.

2. Blending (Assemblage)

Critical step. Achieve consistent house style despite vintage variation. Improve balance (Chardonnay: citrus/finesse/longevity; Pinot Noir: body). Enhance complexity (old reserve wines: dried fruit; oak-matured wines: texture/spice). Ferment many small parcels for maximum blending options.

3. Second Alcoholic Fermentation

Add liqueur de tirage (wine + sugar + yeast + nutrients + clarifying agent). Close with crown cap (plastic cup insert). Stack horizontally in cellars at cool constant temp. Slow fermentation. Raises alcohol ~1.2-1.3% ABV. CO2 dissolves = sparkle. Creates 5-6 atmospheres pressure.

4. Yeast Autolysis

After 2nd fermentation, yeast die and form lees sediment. Over months, yeast cells break down releasing compounds into wine. Contributes bread, biscuit, toast notes. Most active first 4-5 years. Longer on lees = more pronounced yeast character. Maintains freshness for years after autolysis completes.

5. Riddling (Remuage)

Move bottle slowly from horizontal to inverted vertical. Yeast sediment slides into crown cap plastic cup. Traditional: Hand riddling in pupitres (8 weeks). Modern: Gyropalettes (500 bottles, hydraulic arm, completes in days).

6. Disgorgement & Corking

Freeze bottle neck in cold solution. Invert bottle upright. Remove crown cap. Pressure ejects frozen wine plug with sediment. Top up with liqueur d'exp√©dition (wine + sugar = dosage, determines final sweetness). Seal with cork + wire cage. Fully mechanized (seconds). Some wines labeled Brut Nature/Z√©ro Dosage (no sugar added, just wine for top-up).

7. Bottle Ageing

After corking, age few months to integrate liqueur d'exp√©dition. Most ready to drink when released. Some premium wines benefit from further ageing. Note: Cork creates mushroom shape - cylinder compressed before insertion, held by wire cage.

Other Sparkling Wine Methods

Transfer Method

Second fermentation in bottle. Instead of riddling/disgorgement, wine transferred under pressure to tank, filtered, rebottled. Good quality, cheaper than traditional, consistent quality in large batches. Label: "bottle-fermented" (vs "traditional method").

Tank Method (Charmat/Cuve Close)

Second fermentation in sealed pressure tank (not bottle). Retains base wine flavors - ideal for aromatic varieties (Muscat, Riesling) and fruity styles (Prosecco). Large volumes, low price. Cheaper, faster, less labor than bottle fermenting. Base wine: temp-controlled stainless, no MLF/oak. Add yeast/sugar/nutrients, seal tank. Filter before bottling. Usually no autolysis (retains primary fruit). Can create autolytic character by stirring lees with paddles.

Asti Method

For Asti DOCG (Piemonte, Italy). Sweet sparkling from Muscat Blanc √† Petits Grains. Only ONE fermentation. Juice chilled and stored. When needed: warm, ferment in pressure tank. CO2 escapes initially, then tank sealed. Fermentation continues to ~7% ABV and 4-5 atmospheres. Stop by chilling. Filter under pressure. Bottle immediately. Sweet, low alcohol, distinct grapey/peach/floral character. No autolysis. Drink young.

Ancestral Method (P√©tillant Naturel / Pet Nat)

Very old method, recently popular. Single fermentation. Bottle fermenting juice partway through. Captures CO2 from that point. May/may not be disgorged (can be cloudy with sediment if not). Released young. Dry to off-dry (depends if fermentation completes). Low-medium alcohol. Varies in pressure.

Carbonation

CO2 injected into still wine. Bottled under pressure. Cheapest method. Retains base wine flavors. Often used for aromatic varieties (Sauvignon Blanc).

Champagne Styles

Non-Vintage (NV)

Blend of multiple vintages. Represents house style. Most common.

Vintage

Single year. Only best years. Price premium. 3 years minimum on lees.

Blanc de Blancs

100% white grapes (Chardonnay). Lighter body, higher acid, citrus, age-worthy.

Blanc de Noirs

100% black grapes (Pinot Noir/Meunier). Fuller body, red fruit.
